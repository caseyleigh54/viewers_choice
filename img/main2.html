Growing amounts of information. Finite amounts of time.

Every day, the number of documents we encounter increases, while the time we have to understand them decreases. Rather than treating documents as a series of pages in a file, we build tools that focus on ways people will want to engage with the information. Regardless of the type of archive—oral history transcripts, Wikipedia articles, Facebook ads—we deconstruct it into its components (people, events, relationships) using algorithms, and finalize these results with custom curation tools. Approaching the documents as a collection of flexible data points allows us to reconnect the pieces to reveal new ways of seeing and understanding the information.

Our most recent piece, Porfiry, uses the data extracted from court filings and news articles on the Trump-Russia connection to create chronological flows for individual participants. The piece reveals connections that are accessible, but would otherwise be buried. It's an example of what can be done with products we've been developing for understanding documents and archives.

Founded in 2010 by Ben Fry, Fathom Information Design works with clients to understand complex data through interactive tools and software for mobile devices, the web, and large format installations. Out of its studio in Boston, Fathom partners with Fortune 500s and non-profit organizations across sectors, including health care, education, financial services, media, technology, and consumer products.
